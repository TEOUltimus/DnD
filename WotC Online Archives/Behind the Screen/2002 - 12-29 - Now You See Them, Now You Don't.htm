Behind the Screen12/29/2002


Now You See Them, Now You Don't
By Jason Nelson


Back in elementary school, many of us would get certificates on the last day of the school year for "perfect attendance." Whether you were smart, dumb, or anything in between, you could help the school run more smoothly simply by showing up every day on time and doing what you were supposed to do. Nowadays, most gamers have left elementary school far behind and are now busy teens, college students, or working adults. With increased age comes an increased array of demands on your time and usually decreased energy to meet all of them. However much you enjoy a chosen hobby, there will be times when you are either too busy or too tired from your other busy things to make time to game. This is perfectly reasonable and understandable, and no sensible member of a gaming group is going to pitch a fit about people attending to their "real world" responsibilities at the expense of gaming, and few gaming groups expect their members to have "perfect attendance"; even so, it does create problems for the flow of an individual game session and for the campaign as a whole. The purpose of this column is to illuminate some of the issues involved in uneven participation of players and their characters and how a DM can work around the intermittent absence of players and/or their characters and still maintain a quality game for everyone involved.

WORDS TO THE WISE:

1. Perfect attendance is hard to get. Gaming is a leisure activity, so you can't really exert more than peer pressure for players to show up with responsible regularity. Know your players and your group and you can set reasonable expectations for yourself and for them.

2. The Saga Party: The story is all, and the show must go on. Your characters are in it, whether you are there or not.

3. The Virtual Party: The story is important, but player autonomy is also important. Your characters are always around, but when a player isn't, his or her character ends up "offstage" and only pops up at the DM's discretion to handle simple or straightforward campaign issues.

4. The Modular Party: Player control is the final word. If you aren't there, your character isn't either.

5. Is it really such a problem? In some ways, attendance is as much of a problem as your group lets it be a problem. If you all agree on what is expected and what happens with characters when players aren't present, then whatever problems may occur are minimized and livable, even if they are less than ideal.

In the first place, missed or delayed games could be addressed directly among the members of the gaming group. You may have a specific day of the week and/or time of day that you have traditionally played, but as the schedules and life situations of the members of the group change over time, it might be necessary to revisit that issue and determine whether a different meeting time might better fit the needs of the group. People within the group also move away from time to time; while you can't do much when a member makes a long-distance move, if one or more members move within the same city or area, it might be worth considering a move of the game site to one that is more centrally located and convenient for all players. Finally, schedules simply may not permit you to game as often as you once did. You might have played three days a week, ten hours a day when you were in college, but that isn't going to work for working adults. Members who get married and/or have children may spend more time with family and less time at the gaming table. Perhaps a once-a-week game is simply too big a time investment, and an every-other-week or monthly game might ensures better attendance and more cohesion and focus rather than trying to scrape together just enough for a weekly game. All of the above assume that you have group-wide issues with the meeting time. It may be that only one or two people have issues with the time or place of the game, and, if so, the group must decide how far it is willing to go to accommodate such individuals.

These are hard issues to deal with, and, in some ways, they are part of the often unavoidable evolution of your gaming group (sometimes even leading to the dissolution of one group and the formation of another). At the same time, these issues happen relatively infrequently and are fairly obvious. The more common, more subtle, and more insidious issues involving missed gaming time are not part of any large-scale disruption or life change but just an ongoing pattern of regular, day-to-day disruptions. One player has to work late and can't come. Another will be out of town next weekend. Another just got back from a trip and is too tired to play. Another's child got sick and they have to go to the doctor, or they couldn't get babysitting for the game night. Another has a friend in from out of town. All are reasonable, and all are part of normal life -- so exactly what is the problem here?

The problem is that for you, the DM, to structure challenges for a party and to craft interesting storylines for them to pursue, you must know the party that will face these challenges. If the party is not approximately the same from session to session, that obviously makes things difficult. In terms of structuring a quality campaign, you have to forge links between the characters and the world they inhabit. Doing so necessarily requires creating opportunities for the characters to deal with NPCs as people, and adventures or scenarios often end up focused on a specific character and his or her situation. Those familiar with the Al-Qadim setting may recall the "Birthright" adventure in the Ruined Kingdoms boxed set, in which one of the PCs is the long-lost heir of a wealthy merchant, who then must contend with a wrathful stepmother and the son she has raised to be the merchant's heir. Here is a situation rich with roleplaying possibilities and engagement with the inhabitants of the fantasy world, but suppose the adventure starts during one session, but that player cannot make it to the next session. What should you do?

This same principle applies equally well to the more nuts-and-bolts questions of the party's power level. If everyone in the campaign shows up for a session, the party is obviously strong that session. If only two people can make it the next session, they are much weaker. When numbers are few, you also run into balance problems -- the party may have nobody skilled at rogue-oriented abilities and end up stymied by locked doors or traps, or no cleric and be at a disadvantage against undead, or no strong melee person and be vulnerable to close combat. An encounter that you have scaled for one set of characters may be less of a challenge for one combination of characters but overwhelming for a different group. You can fix many of these issues by corrective scaling on the fly, but that is extra work for you. In addition, you just cannot adjust some situations too much without losing their impact in the campaign. If the campaign has been building toward a climactic meeting with the party's nemesis or a tough "final battle" against the leader of the forces of evil or they have completed some dangerous quest and now have returned in triumph to claim their reward from a thankful populace, can you play it out with just a handful of characters without cheapening the value and impact of it? A well-run campaign will flow through crescendo and up to climax, but if the climax is weak, it can taint the memory and feeling about the whole thread leading up to it. It's an empty feeling that can frustrate the DM and players alike when they feel like the dramatic energy of their efforts leading up to the intended climax go for naught -- like air leaking from a ruptured balloon.

A simple solution is to allow characters to simply come and go as their players happen to be present or absent. This has the advantage of expediency, but comes at a high price in believability. If the party has been thrown in the dungeon and the player of Brother Thaddeus the Cleric arrives, does the character suddenly pop up in manacles next to the rest of the party? Is he even aware of what's happened? What if the events that happened would likely not have happened if he had been there? Can he then reasonably be held responsible for participating along with the rest of the party? On the other hand, if the characters are always present and active, front and center stage, regardless of whether the player is there that day, you open up a whole new can of worms about who decides what the character will do, who really controls the character and its creative direction, and on and on.

Taking the above into account, how can you and players alike manage the issue? The most obvious answer is for players to be as conscientious as possible about attending gaming sessions regularly and on-time, or giving notice as far in advance as possible when they can't make it. Despite players' best efforts or intentions, they frequently have to miss games in lieu of more important things, and when that happens, the DM and players need to come to an arrangement about what will happen in their absence. Some of the possible options could include the following:

The Saga Party

In this model, all of the characters always remain present and active. The storyline of the campaign and its internal consistency trumps all other considerations. The characters belong more to the campaign than to the players who created them. If the character's player is not present, then someone else (either another player or you as the DM) will run the character and it will take its lumps and earn its rewards along with the other characters. The show must go on, with or without the players in question (which obviously makes this model less than ideal for players who have a strong investment in their character's behavior and decisions).

The Virtual Party

In this model, the characters are present but take no active role unless their player is present. In a manner of speaking, they are always around somewhere in the vicinity of the action taking place "onstage" in the campaign, but their activities are taking place "offstage."' Their offstage presence enables character-specific campaign elements to be plausibly maintained without forcing players to surrender control of their characters. When they are not there, their characters are simply assumed to be busy doing other things (guarding the horses or prisoners, taking care of personal business, sleeping, examining the tapestries). If time and effort are not at issue, you, the DM, can allow the party to make use of the abilities of the inactive PCs (for example, healing from a party cleric, or identifying magic items, or making certain skill checks), though this should be watched carefully to avoid abuse. When the player shows up, the player pops into active mode as though he or she was there all along.

The Modular Party

In this model, characters come and go along with their players. When the player is not there, the character is unavailable, plain and simple. When the player arrives, you, as the DM, choose a suitable moment for the PC to appear and connect with the rest of the party. Whoever happens to be there is there; whoever is not is not. (This obviously can create problems with logical discontinuities between one session and the next, but that is the price to be paid for total PC independence.)

None of these is a perfect solution, but each group will have its own opinion on how best to deal with the issue. For most groups, the virtual party is probably the best compromise solution. It lacks the story continuity of the saga party and the total player control of the modular party, but it allows for maintaining a reasonable continuity while still allowing players more or less full control of their characters. Whichever way you arrange things, you will still have disputes over treasure distribution (whose character was present for a given mission, adventure, or encounter, and whether that is relevant to your treasure distribution system, as discussed in a previous column), the direction of the campaign, and so on.

The simplest solution, of course, is for everyone to be there all the time, but the expectation of perfect attendance is not realistic. The reason why this is all a problem is quite simply because all gaming sessions are not created equal. Some sessions are simply more important than others in determining the direction of the campaign, sometimes because the encounters in that session have the potential for a Total Party WipeoutTM -- those who show up risk their characters and even the continuation of the campaign, while others are not at risk (and have no opportunity to help). Sometimes the sessions are more important because the party makes a decision about whether to go into the mountains to hunt the Ghost of the Giant-King or head south to investigate the mysteries of Darken Wood. If John Q. Player happens to miss the session where the decision is made, the party may end up going off in a direction he doesn't like. You can dismiss this complaint with the admonition that he should've been there (or sent his vote by email or other proxy), but what if Jimmy J. Player, who happens by only once in a while, showed up that day, so he does get to vote while faithful regular John gets knocked out of the loop? There are no easy solutions to a problem as fundamental as this, but using forethought and good communication can help forestall arguments. As long as everyone understands how the system works, when you're there and when you're not, you probably have a system that everyone at least can live with, and that is usually good enough.

About the Author

Jason Nelson lives in Seattle and is a full-time homemaker (taking care of his 7-year-old daughter, 4-year-old son, and new dog), a part-time graduate student (working on a Ph.D. in education policy), and an active and committed born-again Christian. He runs a weekly D&D game, having just completed a seven-year-long AD&D (heavily modified) campaign set in the Forgotten Realms.
