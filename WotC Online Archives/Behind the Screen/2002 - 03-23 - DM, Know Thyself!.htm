Behind the Screen03/23/2002


DM, Know Thyself!
By Jason Nelson


In the initial installment of Behind the Screen, we discussed how to build a foundation for a good campaign by ensuring that communication takes place among all of the players, including the DM. In that spirit, it is important for you as a DM to get to know the one person with whom you will spend the most time gaming -- yourself. There are things about the way you game and why you game that only you know, and there also may be some things that you never know unless you ask someone else to point them out to you. In any case, bringing the varying facets of your gaming mindset to the foreground can help you account for them and either accentuate them (if positive) or work around them (if negative).

In the category of things that only you know about yourself lays the realm of what you enjoy in running a game, and conversely what you dread about running a game. In thinking about different elements of the game, you might lean a bit on the wisdom of Gary Gygax, one of the co-creators of the D&D game. He recently wrote a series of columns in Dragon Magazine describing survey data he had garnered on what kinds of things gamers -- and gamemasters in particular -- felt were important in a good game or that they enjoyed as a part of their game. Whether or not you fit the typical profile he encountered is not really important; the point is that these categories (see below for examples) provide a useful conceptual framework for ordering your thinking about the parts of the gaming (and DMing) experience. With some kind of classification system in mind, you can sort different parts of the game you run and look at each "bin" of sorted parts to see which parts you really enjoy. As an example, you might divide up a campaign based on the old Against the Giants adventures like this:

1. Hacking: Kill giants, kill more giants, kill ogres and trolls, kill giants, and so on.

2. Magical Combat: Fight with ogre magi. Have combats in dragon lairs and evil drow temples.

3. Mystery: Why the alliance between different giant races? What's with the weird abandoned temples and strange altars? What are those secret messages in elven script? What are they really trying to do?

4. Exotic Locations: We go to the deep forest and a large-scale fortress, a frozen mountain glacier and caves, a volcanic slag-heap, and the Underdark. How do we get the party from one place to another? Magic? Overland journey? What might happen along the way?

5. Full-Scale War: Do we play out the giant invasion? Are these giants all that are involved, or are these just their main bases of operation? Does the war continue while the PCs are attacking the giant bases? What is the giant response to the attacks of the PCs? More war?

6. Sociopolitical: How and why do the PCs get entangled in the whole giant affair? Are they asked by a friendly noble? Commanded by a royal edict? Simply swept up in the middle of the invasion? Have the giants attacked the PCs' homeland? A friendly nation nearby? What do other nations (or races) think about it?

7. Puzzles: How well-hidden should things be? Have PCs earned their loot by bashing the monsters, or do they have to find it too? How cunning should the giants really be, even with outside help? Should they place traps and ambushes? Should apparent treasures and combats be decoys?

8. Allies: Should the PCs expect help from NPCs or creatures they might meet? Are there captives to be rescued, and can they be of any assistance in the war effort?

9. Interaction: How much occasion for conversation will PCs have with the creatures they encounter? Will you even need names for the bad guys, or are they simply sword fodder? Are all of the bad guys equally bad? How do they interact with each other?

10. Timing: How important is the passage of time? Can the PCs raid the giant lairs' at their leisure? Will there be opportunities to rest, resupply, seek guidance or training from others, or do anything else during the adventure? What else will be happening in the world while the PCs undertake the adventure?

In thinking about questions like these, you can sort out the kinds of questions you really want to deal with and which ones you would prefer to skip entirely. If you enjoy exploring the impact of adventure scenarios on the world around the PCs, you might put a heavy emphasis on the sociopolitical dimension, and you may or may not want to actually play out the course of the war "on-stage," so to speak. You can decide how far along the war has come and then briefly narrate the events for their benefit. This can provide context for the sociopolitical setting. In the course of dealing with the local authorities and whatever their own investigations may reveal, the PCs may get a dawning sense of the mystery element, at least a basic notion of what allies they have, and presumably some sense of the exotic locales involved. Then they can pursue the adventure to its fruition: infiltrating the giant lairs and undoing their alliance from within (by a campaign of extermination if necessary). Of course, you may decide to waive the entire background and have the PCs come upon one of the giant lairs while traveling through the wilderness, with little or no idea of what the giants may be up to. The giants may be hostile, or they may think the PCs are in league with their "advisors," which could lead to a bounty of interaction or a bacchanalia of hacking, depending on how it goes. In other words, you can take a given scenario (or even an entire gaming environment, like a nation, world, or plane of existence) and divide it up any number of ways; two different gaming groups might play the same printed adventure but have wildly different experiences because of the parts that you, as DM, saw fit to emphasize. There is nothing at all wrong with that. In fact, it can help your enjoyment (and quite probably that of your players at the same time) if you can look at an adventure idea, whether it is your own creation, something bought off the rack, or an amalgam of the two, and decide which parts appeal to you and which do not.

Taking all of this into account, however, you should not let your enthusiasm (or lack thereof) be the sole determining factor in what you run and how you run it. You may very well enjoy some aspects of the game but lack the skill to carry them off, and that can put a crimp in the enjoyment of the other participants. This is where good communication is essential, because only by talking to your players can you complete your internal checklist of your strengths and weaknesses as a DM. No matter how much you love the idea of magical combat, if it turns the game into a quagmire of rules referencing, you might want to consider de-emphasizing that element until you have boned up on tactical applications of the rules. Likewise, the sociopolitical elements or dealing with allies that you enjoy may be crushingly dull for players if overdone in quantity (no matter how good it might be, there is only so much room in a gamer's mind for imaginary history and politics) or poorly handled in quality (most of the supposed allies turn out to be backstabbing doppelgangers or wererats, for example). Maybe everything is mostly fine, but the campaign just doesn't feel right because nothing ever happens unless the party is there, and time seems to be perpetually frozen on May 2nd on a partly sunny day.

It is important to remember that players can also point out what they think you do very well but where perhaps you sell yourself short. Maybe your descriptions of exotic locales and overland journeys are evocative and compelling, really making the players feel the experience, but you spend most of your time stuffing them into endless 10-foot-wide dungeon corridors. Maybe you are capable of fiendishly clever puzzles or engaging mysteries, but drop them in occasionally as one-offs instead of weaving them into the full tapestry of the campaign. Whatever your strengths and weaknesses may be, you may be the last person in the world to be aware of them, so use your players as a sounding board to see what you are doing well and where you need work. Combining input from others on what you do well with your own knowledge of what you enjoy, you can work to maximize your strong suits and make sure those parts of your game remain not just good but memorable.

At the same time, if you discover that things you don't do very well also happen to be things you don't enjoy, one simple solution is to ditch (or at least minimize) those elements entirely from your game. If your players don't particularly enjoy these elements, this is probably the best solution. On the other hand, you might consider the idea that you (and perhaps your players) have not enjoyed these elements because you have never done them well. In that case, you could try to work out a new way of approaching that part of the game by talking to other DMs (possibly including some of your players) about how they handle those elements of the game. Playing a character in someone else's campaign is an excellent way to get a view from "the other side" and see how DMs handle different game elements. You may pick up some ideas that give you the impetus to try again with a fresh approach. When you solicit comments and suggestions from your players and other gamers, and when you view them as formative tools (rather than as some sort of criticism or challenge), you can greatly improve the quality of your campaign.
